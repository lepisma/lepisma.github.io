How to Identify Human-Generated Content? First ask Why

ai technology ml llm

I have complete confidence that machines can generate content indistinguishable from humans. This is coming after being in the field of Machine Learning for quite some time, making systems that interact with humans while trying to pass as other humans. No, I am not declaring victory (or defeat) of any sort. I am just trying to say that any data that's representable digitally is going to be machine replicable in time, and that's been true forever. Proving this is not the point of this post. Here I want to focus on this representability aspect and how that's orthogonal from what we mean when we say we want to see content by humans on the Internet.



On the most extreme side of machine's ability deniers, we see people invoking some sort of innate and non-reproducible linguistic uniqueness that humans have which will always lead to differences in what humans create and what machines could. Then we come to weaker forms of this that go like "While machines can theoretically replicate humans, practically there will always be some difference." This camp, at the moment, talks about things like how AI text is devoid of soul and creativity and this and that. All of this without knowing that most of the texts that we humans generate are devoid of soul and creativity and this and that. Does that mean they are non-human? We can't use a meticulously and beautifully crafted piece of text and say that's how a general human generated piece of text looks like. If you can put this soul and creativity in digital form, according to me, AI can also generate content with soul and creativity. The question then is, can you represent a person's humanity in any other form?

1. True humanity is Unrepresentable

When I was working on customer care voicebots, one of the key problems that occupied our minds in the ML team was representing and passing on fine-grained details of the caller's voice to our models so they could return more value for the business. This involved a lot of interesting paralinguistics work and usually generated interests in two camps:

  1. Hype echo-chambers involving non-technical leaders, VCs, advisors, etc.
  2. Researchers who wanted to truly understand what goes inside human communication.

The unaffected camp, say camp 3, was of the actual builders who understood the core problem and kept focusing on solving them rather than asking for higher accuracy in detecting caller emotions. While this is a common story for any hyped-up technology, I want you to observe the difference between camp 2 and 3. If you are able to break free of dichotomies, you could see that their beliefs, interests, motivations, and efforts are all orthogonal.

I believe when we ask for true humanity, it's—by design—asking for something other than just higher fidelity representations. We keep hearing that this something else is merely a matter of missing representation, specially covering what's always beyond current capabilities. When we were building text chatbots, the missing human was in the voice. When we started working with voice—where we used to get a choppy 8khz sampled signal along with some metadata on a channel—the missing human was outside this channel sitting in a room somewhere dealing with emotions that we don't know of. Regardless of what we do with the available channel, the human always lies outside of it. Even if you have thoughts transferred directly in computer representable form, we would arguably say that the true human is hidden coiled in the inner thoughts that our machine didn't read from the brain scan1.

But, the whole point of human content creation process is to create something that establishes a provenance to a unique claim of humanity. This claim can only be verified by finding and involving the person claiming it. And that's beyond representation, since you can't simulate a person completely without being the person themselves. Humans are singleton sets.

As for the product we were working on, that didn't need us to figure out true humanity to get going. And that brings me to my next point.

1.1. You don't need Humans, just solutions to problems

For many situations involving representation of human generated content in some form, you don't need the full human. You might not even need a human at all. And where you do need full human, you can't do without knowing one. So if you truly want to know how a massage chair feels to your mom, you have to make her sit on that chair. Internet reviews by humans on platforms will never be a substitute for this. If you just want to buy a decent massage chair in general, well, those reviews will work. As will many other solutions.

There is obviously an intersection here since our current solution for trustworthy buys is based on human reviews. But this change of perspective will make you worry differently about an AI generated review instead of a human written one. The problem is not so much that AIs are impersonating humans, but that you are not getting trustworthy information to make buying decisions.

1.2. Oh, and you also need Humans

I have enjoyed listening to music that my friends have personally recommended, and I have also enjoyed algorithmic suggestions. But I keep it clear in my mind that an algorithmic friend persona will always be different than a human friend here, even if they suggest the same songs to me. Exactly because when I ask a human for songs, I also seek the human in some ways.

When you truly need human content, you will always look beyond the channel. If you don't need it, the channels will be just fine. AI generated letters could be totally okay unless you truly wanted someone to put the effort to write to you. It's not about quality and definitely not about how nicely can an AI replicate that person's handwriting.

2. Where am I going with all this?

This post comes out as a rant so I will take some time to tie things up:

  1. Digitally representable data implies digital replicability.
  2. Humans are (and their humanity is) unrepresentable in the full, even with embodied agents. There is no mysticism here, it follows by definition.
  3. Most times, you just need higher quality content, and you should try figuring out how to get it2 without tying yourself to a source.
  4. When you really need human content, you have to seek out the actual humans behind them. There is no way around it.

This whole epistemological fuss about whether a generated text is by an AI or human will blow over as models get better. And we will end up with the real question in this debate: Why do you want human content over AI? And the answer will not involve capability, creativity, embodiment, or any higher power, but simply a cultural affinity to true human provenance.

I believe the work on identifying AI or human content will move towards a dynamic equilibrium not unlike fraud detection. And very similar to fraud detection, you will realize that the problem is not so much about AI or human but right or wrong (even useful or useless). Large content platforms will continue abusing AI. Mixed systems will continue in the chaos of wisdom-of-crowd systems to build trust. Small niche groups will keep popping up and out of existence with artisanal handcrafted produces. But we, poor lost souls, need to understand what we are looking for when we say we like non-algorithmic, human, content.

It doesn't matter if, say, I am a movie reviewer but an AI is doing million times better than me and all my friends who used to watch me are now watching the AI. In fact, if they choose the AI, they did not reject me (or humans in general); they just selected higher quality content. If they were really watching me, they will continue watching me. I mentioned this earlier but it's worth repeating again: All of what we connect with humans is about individuals as singletons. If another entity or AI is going through the exact same life experience as me, down to the tiniest details, then that AI system is me.

I know that it's a strong assumption to say that everyone who decides to choose content thinks deeply about the source. I am also not acknowledging the control large media systems have over our own interests and motivations. But that's a part of my humanity. You can better this post in so many ways, but it will never be my post written at this specific time and place. And if that's alright with you, you didn't need to know my view, you needed a high quality view.

And I believe accepting that sometimes we want higher quality things that could be from machines and lower quality things just because they are from humans, is a remarkably human trait.
