The Problem with Modelling Subjectivity

ml llm writing

Modelling a subjective variable mapping human opinions or creativity is objectively more difficult than objective entities like correctness of logical arguments, result of a numerical computation, etc. Aggregating and using agreement scores among raters can help but only in cases where the aggregate itself makes any sense. And many times we don't think making sense makes sense.

1. Subjectivity

In Machine Learning practice, we often overload the term subjectivity. Sometimes disagreements in ground truth is assumed to be caused by subjectivity. Other times just plain uncertainty in a variable makes people invoke subjectivity. Of course the actual subjective entities like human preferences and interpretations are also called subjective.

When there is an underlying objective truth, these difference-of-opinion subjectivities are just estimation errors. Disagreements and voting usually helps to get rid of these errors on both sides and you tend to get closer to the truth in the classic Wisdom of the Crowd style. Here is one of the origin stories around this (from the Wikipedia page):

The classic wisdom-of-the-crowds finding involves point estimation of a continuous quantity. At a 1906 country fair in Plymouth, 800 people participated in a contest to estimate the weight of a slaughtered and dressed ox. Statistician Francis Galton observed that the median guess, 1207 pounds, was accurate within 1% of the true weight of 1198 pounds.[7] This has contributed to the insight in cognitive science that a crowd's individual judgments can be modeled as a probability distribution of responses with the median centered near the true value of the quantity to be estimated.[8]

Now consider a case like figuring out emotions from a spoken utterance. While you could be in the ballpark when you say a speech utterance is sounding \(80\%\) happy, \(20\%\) confused, and \(0\%\) sad, you can't be exactly right since the truth itself is a probability distribution over emotions. Asking many people to listen to the audio and giving their own estimates could make your aggregate output more robust but it still doesn't change the inner fuzziness of the truth which you will have to account for in the interpretation anyway.

Keep making the truth hazier, and you move more and more towards actual subjectivity. These are cases where we start using words like preferences and interpretations. Favorite color of various people is one such thing. There are a lot of context that goes behind people's choices of colors and even with strong cross-cultural commonalities, it will be stupid to say that there is a single hidden true favorite color. You can again make a probability distribution over colors like in the case of emotions, but that will be very different since you will then be studying the aggregate itself and not the individual favorites.

The last two cases involve different subjectivities as one is about color preference of all humans and the other is about perceived emotions of one single human utterance. The latter having more objective look and feel to it. In reality things fall somewhere in between, but we tend to push them on one side.

2. The Average Human

I recently found out about LitBench (Fein, Daniel and Russo, Sebastian and Xiang, Violet and Jolly, Kabir and Rafailov, Rafael and Haber, Nick, 2025) that attempts to set up a benchmark of creativity in writing by trying to use upvotes by users on the content from /r/WritingPrompts subreddit. Now while they do acknowledge that creativity is subjective, they make the human-collective view the only object of study and don't give much attention to individual views that, in my opinion, are the source of subjectivity in this case. Of course broadly an article \(A\) could be more creative than article \(B\) according to most of the humans, but using a collective vote as the only representation, specially for ambiguous cases, misinterprets the subjectivity involved in creativity.

To make a caricature of this, it's like saying the batsmen in cricket play at a 60-100° angle instead of either 0° or 180° since 30-50% of batsmen are left-handed1. It doesn't make sense. The ratio of left-handed batsmen is a meaningful variable, but projecting that ratio to the angle of stance is absurd.

2.1. Distributions are important

To be fair to LitBench team, they do state in places that they are trying to model the popularity of reddit posts using the upvotes as the proxy for creativity evaluation. Here is a clip from the limitations section of the paper:

A key limitation of our work stems from the assumption, inherited from [Ethayarajh et al., 2023], that upvotes on Reddit contain information about human preferences. Though we experimentally validate our dataset via human evaluation, it is unclear what other underlying factors may be encoded in upvote information. For example, [Kassaeyan, 2016] report that the decision to upvote a post on social networks is at least partially driven by personal and social mechanisms, including individuation, perceived behavioral control, and altruism.



Our extension of human preferences to determine writing quality is further complicated by the question of subjectivity in writing evaluation. There is a body of work showing how measured features of writing can correlate with human ratings in aggregate [Zedelius et al., 2019, McNamara et al., 2010]. Moreover, authorship on fair writing evaluation in a classroom setting establishes precedent for objectivity in writing [Weigle, 2002].

This makes more sense, but then I find it the title of the paper, "LitBench: A Benchmark and Dataset for Reliable Evaluation of Creative Writing", to be misleading at best, and clickbait at worst.

If you make a model that does perfectly on LitBench, it's not likely to be a supremely humanlike judge of creative writing. Even keeping aside what reddit upvotes mean, in my opinion, such cases need us to learn individual distributions. Upvotes are not independent in the sense that each user on that subreddit votes in a certain pattern that projects2 their preferences and understanding of creativity or whatever that subreddit upvotes map to. And an aggregation of these doesn't imply any truer estimate of creativity, unless creativity is actually an objective value like the weight of the Galtonian Ox. And creativity is not the weight of an Ox. It involves probabilistic perception of a probability distribution. Consider novelty, one of the core components of perceived creativity, which is heavily dependent on historical context of the reader. For a joke to land well on the novelty dimension and be considered creative in that way, the listeners shouldn't have heard similar jokes a thousand times before already. Now think how to judge a joke completely based on voting over the textual content.

Assume \(c_{ix}\) is the variable representing creativity attributed by person \(i\) to a writing \(x\). This variable has its own distribution.

Models that understand creative writing similar to humans will be good at modelling \(P_i = \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_x [c_{ix}]\) and not \(P(x) = \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_i [c_{ix}]\). An ideal AI creativity rater will gel well in the list of \(i\) human raters (or a subset of them to engage expertise). This could be done by either passing a unique id to the model or some individual context and history. You should basically be able to swap out all users on the subreddit with rating bots and there should be no change in the feed ranking. And this is because the bots are acting similarly to humans in their diverse and subjective preference of writings.

In the legacy EQ-Bench paper (Samuel J. Paech, 2023), emotions were rated based on similarity to the scores given by one person (the author). In some ways, models doing good on that are capturing truer human subjectivity—of that author, in this case—than a smoothed out collective scores.

In the end, maybe the collective creativity ratings are the only things needed to make useful products. Many practically systems work on aggregates of human votes in the real world. But that's not the complete picture. Models can interface better with individual humans when their consistencies or inconsistencies follow that of other humans. And that's going to be important in building helpful and understandable AI assistance.

In the meantime I hope these differences are called out and used wisely in communication, technical or otherwise, lest we end up degrading the semantics of core human abilities like creativity and emotions. I believe research in AI should help us gain deeper insights by spending a lot of time questioning the foundational objects of study as compared to merely running GPUs and placing scores in a table.
