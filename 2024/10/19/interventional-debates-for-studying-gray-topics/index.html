Interventional Debates for Studying Gray Topics

learning llm 100daystooffload

A lot of people these days are talking about NotebookLM, specially its audio overview feature that generates a podcast style audio recording from your sources. While I understand this is more engaging than calling a summary function on the content, I am not a fan.

Most of what I like about learning about new topics using LLMs involves being active in the process by asking narrow and specific questions than ingesting a prepared information dump. Interview style podcasts use questions too but since they are not your questions you can instead go read (which I prefer) an article, probably more actively.

For topics that are gray—where you don't know all aspects well enough to explore the whole territory just based on your own questions—you need interactivity but also assistance. Let's say I want to study 'Capitalism in India'. I can't do much by only asking questions to an LLM since I don't have a good sense what all context to cover, which era of the country to study more, how to compare Indian Capitalism to Western, etc. I also can't just open Wikipedia and read everything around Indian Economics since I might have specific inclinations around my motivation which will not be met with inefficient hops on wiki pages. Another example topic is the whole Israel vs Palestine tension. Here too I need expert assistance in asking the right questions to understand everything.

There are many techniques to attack such ambiguous and multifaceted gray topics. Here is a rough map that, according to me, shows involvement of a student (active to passive) and the effectiveness of study:

We can do better. Looking at the graph, the best way to efficiently study here would be to sit as a moderator in a debate of experts. I don't have experts for everything but I can have cheap copies via LLMs. Because of this dirty approximation I would have to be more intervening in this debate as a moderator. I am calling this technique Interventional Debate (ID) and would put it on the more active side of the spectrum like shown below:

I am open to changes in the maps above as I understand more about what works how, so take this with a pinch of salt.

1. idli

To try out ID, I made a small tool called idli1 that lets me set up a debate on a topic between auto-generated debaters where I can intervene at any point and do things like steer the conversation in a direction, check facts (pretty important since LLMs will bluff), call out logical fallacies, ask for clarifications, read about something that was brought up in the debate, etc.

Here is a log of and ID on a dummy topic 'Dark vs Light theme for programming' to show how this works. Note that only the topic and texts under 'Moderator' are written by me:

This is a debate between 2 debaters on the above topic. To start members will

** Debater A

comfortable coding experience. Moreover, the contrast in light themes mirrors
traditional mediums like paper, facilitating smoother transitions and reducing
cognitive load. Embracing a light theme can subsequently lead to increased
productivity and decreased eye strain, as it aligns with the natural environment
humans are biologically accustomed to.

** Debater B

fatigue. This is because dark backgrounds emit less light, which reduces the
glare that can strain eyes when staring at bright screens for long
periods. Moreover, on OLED and AMOLED screens, dark themes conserve battery life
by lighting up fewer pixels, thus enhancing energy efficiency. Additionally, the
subdued color palette of dark themes can foster a focused, immersive coding
environment, especially in dimly lit settings, by minimizing distractions and
keeping code elements distinct, thereby boosting productivity and concentration.

** Debater A
While dark themes may reduce eye strain in low-light settings, light themes
excel in providing superior readability during daylight hours, a crucial aspect
for those who code throughout the day. The natural alignment of light themes

seamless working environment. Adopting light themes can bridge the gap between
digital and traditional reading habits, leading to less mental transition and a
more intuitive coding process. Furthermore, the consistent contrast of dark text
on a light background reduces the risk of headaches and eye fatigue over long
coding sessions, enhancing both comfort and productivity.

** Debater B

vision and sleep quality, especially when working late hours or in dim
environments. This ergonomic consideration, combined with the modern aesthetic
appeal of dark interfaces, can lead to healthier long-term coding habits and a
more modern, sleek development experience.
